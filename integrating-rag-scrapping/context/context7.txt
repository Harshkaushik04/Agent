Samay Raina (born 26 October 1997)[1] is an Indian stand-up comedian and YouTuber. He was the co-winner of the stand-up comedy contest Comicstaan 2 (2019).[2] In 2024, he hosted the comedy talent show India's Got Latent.
Raina was born in Jammu city of the erstwhile Indian state of Jammu and Kashmir into a conservative Kashmiri Pandit family from Salia area of Seer Hamdan Anantnag. He enrolled in a print engineering course at Vidhyarthi Griha's College of Engineering and Technology in Pune, Maharashtra, which he said was a waste of time, and started doing open mic events and eventually became a regular in the local comedy scene.[3]
After performing at multiple open mics since 27 August 2017, Raina began opening for well-known comedians like Anirban Dasgupta and Abhishek Upmanyu in Pune. As he gained recognition, he moved to Mumbai to pursue a career in stand-up comedy and gave several successful shows in Mumbai and several cities across the nation.[4]
Eventually, he joined Comicstaan 2 from a suggestion from his co-winner Aakash Gupta. Later, he became the joint winner of Comicstaan 2 with Aakash Gupta, aired on Amazon Prime Video.[5][6]
During the COVID-19 pandemic, the government cancelled all outdoor events, and thus, Raina could no longer perform stand-up comedy. He then began streaming chess games on his YouTube channel at the fellow comedian Tanmay Bhat's suggestion.[7] His viewership boosted when he invited YouTuber Antonio Radiƒá, popularly known as Agadmator, to his channel. In response to this, Indian GM Vidit Gujrathi tweeted that he would like to join Raina on his track. Eventually, Gujrathi appeared on Raina's channel, giving another boost to Raina's viewership. Since then, the two have fostered a strong relationship and frequently feature on each other's channels. Gujrathi attributes his moving from Twitch to YouTube to Raina's suggestion.[citation needed]
According to many chess proponents, including GMs Viswanathan Anand, Vidit Gujrathi, Anish Giri, Teimour Radjabov, Baskaran Adhiban, Emil Sutovsky and IMs Tania Sachdev and Sagar Shah, Raina has been instrumental in popularising chess in India through his channel.[8] Raina tries to make the game of chess more appealing to the masses through his humour which, according to many, has helped him reach a larger audience.[7]
Several well-known chess personalities like Pentala Harikrishna, Judit Polg√°r, former world chess champions Vladimir Kramnik, Viswanathan Anand and Magnus Carlsen have appeared on Raina's channel.[9][10] He also organised chess tournaments featuring Indian celebrities such as cricketer Yuzvendra Chahal,[11] and played chess with actor Aamir Khan.[12]
In addition to chess, he also live streams many other games on his YouTube channel.
Samay plays on Chess.com as samayraina. As of 6 December 2024[update], Raina has a rating of 1621 at rapid chess on chess.com, while the highest recorded rating was 1942 on 3 August 2023.[13] On 5 May 2021, Raina won the $10,000 Botez Bullet Invitational, a one-hour amateur bullet arena tournament sponsored by Chess.com and hosted by the Botez sisters. He was the only Indian streamer competing among some of the top international Twitch streamers and earned $4000.[14]
He started organising online chess tournaments on his channel to attract the online audience to chess, called Comedians On Board (COB), inviting many of his comedian friends and other celebrities.[15]
Raina with ChessBase India and Nodwin Gaming organised an online chess league called Chess Super League consisting of some of the Top International and Indian Grandmasters, International Masters and Indian Junior Players, competing in six teams of six players each. For the prize pool of ‚Çπ40 lakh (US$47,000). The entire event was streamed on Raina's YouTube Channel.[22]
During the COVID-19 pandemic, he began streaming chess games along with multiple comedians and chess masters. He has raised substantial amounts of money for various causes through his YouTube channel, including help for waste pickers,[23] and relief for West Bengal and Assam flood victims.[24][25]
In June 2024, Samay Raina launched India's Got Latent, a reality show focused on uncovering hidden talents.[26][27][28]
He then ventured into the tech and entertainment space with the launch of his app India's Got Latent, inspired by his YouTube show. Raina said he aims to eventually transition the platform into a full-fledged OTT service, featuring shows across genres such as poetry, rap, and reality TV. Within hours of launch, the app reached top of the charts on Apple App Store and Google Play Store.[29]

Streaming is how you watch and listen to media (video, audio, or live events) directly over the internet without having to download it. Instead of downloading the content file on your device, streaming delivers it in real time while you watch or listen. For example, you watch movies or series on Netflix without downloading them. An audio content example would be Spotify where you listen to songs or podcasts without saving them on your device. How does streaming work? Here is a step-by-step process on how streaming works: 1. Requesting content When start playing a video or music track, your device sends a request to the server that hosts the content. The server had the media file stored and is ready to dispense it as soon as you request it. Chunking the data Rather than sending the whole media file at once (which could take more time, storage, and consume internet), the server splits it into smaller pieces called ‚Äúpackets.‚Äù Each packet represents a small part of the video or audio (worth a few seconds), making the process faster and easier to manage. 2. Data transmission After the server breaks down the data, it starts sending these packets to your device. These data packets travel across the internet through various protocols and networks to ensure they arrive at your device in the correct order and format. 3. Buffering Your device doesn‚Äôt start playing the content with the first packets that arrive. Instead, it temporarily stores a few seconds of data in a buffer, which helps to keep playback smooth even if your internet briefly slows down. Excessive buffering, however, isn‚Äôt the part of process, it happens due to multiple reasons that we‚Äôve discussed in our guide here. 4. Decoding and playback Once the buffer has enough data, the media player begins to decode the packets. Since these packets contain compressed data, your device uses encoding techniques (like AAC for audio or H.264 for audio) to decompress it back into the original format. As a result, the media starts playing smoothly through your speakers or on your screen. 5. Continuous data flow As you stream, your device keeps sending requests for more data to the server. As the server sends the data packets, your device continually adds them to the buffer. The content will play smoothly as long as the buffer is full. 6. Adaptive bitrate streaming Your streaming platform will adjust the quality of the stream to match your bandwidth (whether your internet connection slows down or speeds up). For example, if your internet connection slows down, the video bitrate quality might drop from HD to SD to prevent buffering and ensure the stream runs smoothly. Conversely, the stream will automatically switch to high quality if the connection improves. 7. Content delivery networks (CDNs) Most platforms use content delivery networks (CDNs) to ensure the streaming process is faster and more efficient. These are servers spread across the world, close to the user. So, when you‚Äôre streaming something online, the data doesn‚Äôt always originate from the main server. Instead, it comes from the nearest video CDN server, which shortens the distance the data travels, reducing buffering and making the streaming faster. 8. Streaming ends Once you‚Äôre done watching or listening, the server stops sending data packets to your device. Any leftover packets in your buffer may be saved temporarily in your device‚Äôs cache. If you want to watch the same content again, the process starts over ‚Äì but it might load faster because some data is still stored in the cache. Streaming is how you watch and listen to media (video, audio, or live events) directly over the internet without having to download it. Instead of downloading the content file on your device, streaming delivers it in real time while you watch or listen.
For example, you watch movies or series on Netflix without downloading them. An audio content example would be Spotify where you listen to songs or podcasts without saving them on your device.
Here is a step-by-step process on how streaming works:
When start playing a video or music track, your device sends a request to the server that hosts the content. The server had the media file stored and is ready to dispense it as soon as you request it.
Rather than sending the whole media file at once (which could take more time, storage, and consume internet), the server splits it into smaller pieces called ‚Äúpackets.‚Äù Each packet represents a small part of the video or audio (worth a few seconds), making the process faster and easier to manage.
After the server breaks down the data, it starts sending these packets to your device. These data packets travel across the internet through various protocols and networks to ensure they arrive at your device in the correct order and format.
Your device doesn‚Äôt start playing the content with the first packets that arrive. Instead, it temporarily stores a few seconds of data in a buffer, which helps to keep playback smooth even if your internet briefly slows down.
Excessive buffering, however, isn‚Äôt the part of process, it happens due to multiple reasons that we‚Äôve discussed in our guide here.
Once the buffer has enough data, the media player begins to decode the packets. Since these packets contain compressed data, your device uses encoding techniques (like AAC for audio or H.264 for audio) to decompress it back into the original format. As a result, the media starts playing smoothly through your speakers or on your screen.
As you stream, your device keeps sending requests for more data to the server. As the server sends the data packets, your device continually adds them to the buffer. The content will play smoothly as long as the buffer is full.
Your streaming platform will adjust the quality of the stream to match your bandwidth (whether your internet connection slows down or speeds up). For example, if your internet connection slows down, the video bitrate quality might drop from HD to SD to prevent buffering and ensure the stream runs smoothly. Conversely, the stream will automatically switch to high quality if the connection improves.
Most platforms use content delivery networks (CDNs) to ensure the streaming process is faster and more efficient. These are servers spread across the world, close to the user. So, when you‚Äôre streaming something online, the data doesn‚Äôt always originate from the main server.
Instead, it comes from the nearest video CDN server, which shortens the distance the data travels, reducing buffering and making the streaming faster.
Once you‚Äôre done watching or listening, the server stops sending data packets to your device. Any leftover packets in your buffer may be saved temporarily in your device‚Äôs cache. If you want to watch the same content again, the process starts over ‚Äì but it might load faster because some data is still stored in the cache.

India's Got Latent is a fresh addition to the Indian talent show landscape, offering a unique spin on traditional reality competitions. Created and hosted by comedian Samay Raina, the show provides a platform for contestants to showcase diverse talents, ranging from singing and dancing to beatboxing and stand-up comedy. Inspired by the popular Kill Tony podcast from Austin, Texas, the show stands out for its unpredictable and humorous format.
The show premiered on June 14, 2024, and is primarily available on YouTube, making it accessible to a wide audience. Full episodes can be streamed for free on Samay Raina's YouTube channel, while exclusive content is restricted to paid members. Additionally, an official India's Got Latent app offers behind-the-scenes footage and extended unfiltered content.
Unlike conventional talent shows, India's Got Latent embraces a self-rating system, where contestants predict their performance scores before stepping on stage. If their rating aligns with the judges' average, they win. This unique format adds an element of self-awareness and strategy, making the show unpredictable and engaging.

Diverse Talent: From stunts to quirky performances, the show brings fresh acts to the spotlight.
